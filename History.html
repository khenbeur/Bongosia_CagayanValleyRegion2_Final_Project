    • History of Cagayan Valley

    The history of the Cagayan Valley (Region II) is deeply rooted in both ancient human habitation and crucial colonial development. Archaeological evidence, including the discovery of Homo luzonensis fossils in Callao Cave and extensive Shell Middens, shows that the region was settled by various groups, most notably the riverine Ibanag people, who established thriving trade networks long before European contact. Spanish colonization began in earnest in the late 16th century, establishing the initial capital, Ciudad Nueva Segovia (Lal-lo), and initiating widespread evangelization led by Dominican friars, resulting in the iconic red-brick churches seen throughout the area.

    This colonial period was defined by the lucrative Tobacco Monopoly imposed by the Spanish government starting in the late 18th century. The mandated cultivation of tobacco transformed the valley's economy, leading to significant wealth for the crown but also sparking resistance and attracting massive Ilocano migration from the west. This influx eventually led to the division of the vast original province into its modern constituents: Cagayan, Isabela, and Nueva Vizcaya, and solidified its identity as a vital agricultural, trading, and cultural heartland of Northern Luzon, blending indigenous Ibanag traditions with Ilocano influence.
